Background & Motivation

Why do words come out in clusters?

Clustering-and-Switching Model

People retrieve words in bursts (clusters) separated by longer pauses at sub-category boundaries. Proposed by Troyer, Moscovitch & Winocur (1997), tested in monolingual English speakers — does it hold for Hindi-English bilinguals?

Lexical Foraging Hypothesis

Semantic memory retrieval resembles foraging: exploit a local patch (cluster) until depleted, then switch. Hills et al. (2012) showed this maps onto graph-theoretic neighbourhood density — we test this directly with SpAM distances.

The Bilingual Angle

Hindi-English bilinguals code-switch mid-sequence. Does Hinglish vs English script choice reflect the language of acquisition? Are retrieval dynamics the same regardless of script?

Why Both Tasks?

VFT measures behaviour (timing, order, fluency). SpAM measures representation (subjective semantic geometry). Together they let us predict retrieval speed from semantic neighbourhood structure — a complete loop.

Core conjecture: a word's retrieval speed in VFT is not just about familiarity — it depends on where it sits in the shared semantic map and how densely populated its local neighbourhood is.

Data Engineering

From Raw Clicks to Analysis-Ready CSV

responses.json ← custom web experiment; one entry per session; nested word + timestamp arrays
↓ parse timestamps → compute IRT (inter-response time)
↓ tag language_type (Hindi/Hinglish vs English)
vft_responses.csv ← 35 participants × 4 domains × N words; cols: subject_id, domain, word, rt_ms, position, language_type
↓ join on subject_id + domain + word with SpAM (x, y) coordinates
merged_vft_spam_responses.csv ← VFT + SpAM integrated; adds cols: x (0–1), y (0–1)

IRT Definition

Gap (ms) between successive word outputs. First word IRT = time from trial start. Right-skewed: most <3,000 ms; tail to 10,000+ ms marks cluster switches.

SpAM Coordinates

Participants dragged word tokens on a 2D board. Normalised x, y ∈ [0,1]. Pairwise Euclidean distance = subjective semantic distance.

Consensus Matrix

Averaged pairwise SpAM distances across 35 participants per domain → one 30×30 consensus distance matrix fed into MDS + clustering.

VFT — Key Visualisations

Three Plots That Tell the Full Story

Fig 3 · Serial Position vs IRT

What it shows: IRT (ms) plotted against word position 1→N for each domain. Upward loess curve = lexical exhaustion.

Key inference: Colours exhausted by position ~14 (closed vocabulary); Animals retain low slope far beyond position 20 (multiple sub-clusters). Steeper slope = shallower semantic space.

Fig 4 · Within vs Between Cluster IRT RQ1

What it shows: Grouped bar chart — mean within-cluster IRT vs mean between-cluster IRT per domain. Error bars = SE.

Key inference: Between-cluster IRT ≈ 1.7× within-cluster IRT. Welch's t, p < 0.05, Cohen's d ≈ 0.5–0.8. Clustering-and-switching model replicated in bilinguals.

Fig 5 · Fluency Score Bubble Chart RQ3

What it shows: Each bubble = one participant. X = mean IRT, Y = total Hindi/Hinglish word count. Bubble size = domains attempted.

Key inference: Significant negative r — faster retrievers produce more words. Mean IRT is a valid individual-level proxy for lexical access efficiency.

RQ4 (not shown): Both mean cluster size and total switch count showed positive correlations with total fluency. High-fluency participants exploit clusters deeply and switch efficiently — both strategies matter.

SpAM Analysis — Semantic Maps

What Does Semantic Memory Look Like?

Animals

Three distinct clusters on MDS map: wild (sher, baag, cheetah), domestic (kutta, billi, gaay), birds (sparrow, crow, parrot). Wide map = high vocabulary diversity. Silhouette optimal k = 3.

Foods

Richest structure. Pulses cluster (dal, chana, rajma) tight and central. Snacks (samosa, maggi) peripheral. Meals (roti, chawal, sabzi) bridging. Highest optimal k of all domains.

Colours

Arranged in a near-perceptual colour wheel arc. Warm tones (laal, peela, naarangi) vs cool (neela, hara, ferozi) vs achromatic (kaala, safed). Tightest consensus of all domains.

Body-parts

Clear internal/external split. Face region (naak, ankh, kaan, munh) forms its own tight cluster. Limbs (haath, paon, ungli) separate. Clinical anatomical terms form periphery.

Heatmap Block Patterns

Dense dark-diagonal blocks in consensus heatmaps confirm sub-categories are real and consistently perceived. A uniform heatmap would mean zero shared structure — our data shows strong agreement.

MDS Semantic Maps

Bubble size = VFT production frequency. Large bubbles at cluster centres are prototypical words. Small peripheral bubbles are atypical/rare. Spatial centrality predicts VFT accessibility.

Part II — Cross-Task Integration

SpAM × VFT

Can semantic neighbourhood structure predict retrieval speed?

RQ2 Hypothesis:
"Words with tighter semantic neighbourhoods (lower mean SpAM distance) should be retrieved faster (lower IRT), because they are surrounded by closely related words that co-activate each other in semantic memory."

Research Question 2 — Deep Dive

Semantic Neighbourhood Density Predicts Retrieval Speed

Hypothesis

Words closer to their semantic neighbours in the SpAM consensus matrix should have lower mean VFT IRT. Co-activation from dense neighbourhoods accelerates retrieval.

Method

For each word: compute mean SpAM neighbourhood distance (mean pairwise distance to all other words) and mean VFT IRT across all participants who produced it. Pooled Pearson r across all 4 domains.

Result

Positive pooled Pearson r — words farther from their semantic neighbourhood took longer to retrieve. Effect strongest for animals & foods (open, rich domains), weaker for colours (closed, uniform domain).

Implication

First within-participant, bilingual-sample evidence for the lexical foraging model. Retrieval speed is not just familiarity — it is topological position in the shared semantic map. Confirms Hills et al. (2012) in a novel population.

Conclusions

What We Found — and What Remains Open

Core Findings

  • Clustering-and-switching is universal: confirmed in Hindi-English bilinguals with Cohen's d 0.5–0.8
  • Lexical foraging has a measurable substrate: SpAM neighbourhood density directly predicts VFT IRT
  • Language of acquisition shapes retrieval: foods/body-parts → Hindi; colours → English
  • Mean IRT is a validated individual-efficiency proxy (RQ3 negative r)
  • Both strategy types matter: depth-of-exploitation and breadth-of-switching predict fluency (RQ4)
  • Semantic maps are consistent across 35 bilinguals — shared cultural-linguistic representations

Limitations & Future Work

  • Web-based self-timed paradigm: IRT includes typing latency — under-estimates true cognitive IRT
  • SpAM top-30 words per domain: infrequent/idiosyncratic words excluded from neighbourhood analysis
  • n = 35 is modest; confidence intervals especially for domain-level r are wide
  • All participants IIIT-H students: high-education, urban, tech-savvy — limits generalisability
  • Cross-sectional: longitudinal VFT+SpAM could track how semantic maps shift with L2 proficiency
  • Future: EEG synchrony during cluster transitions; clinical application to dementia screening
